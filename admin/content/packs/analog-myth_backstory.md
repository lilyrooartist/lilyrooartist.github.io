## Core narrative seed
Analog Myth starts in a classroom with an analog clock on the wall and a quiet admission: Lily and her friends could not really read it. If they needed the actual time, they checked a Chromebook or asked the kid with an Apple Watch.

That Apple Watch kid becomes almost mythic in the song's world: the person with the truth strapped to their wrist. The wall clock is official, but the wearable is trusted. The classroom says time is obvious; the students quietly build a workaround.

The title track turns that mismatch into the album's thesis. If nobody can read the clock, does the clock still control the room? If one watch gives certainty and two watches create doubt, what happens when you have a wall clock, a Chromebook, an Apple Watch kid, and a general sense of doom?

Musically, the violin gives the song a darker, haunted-clock edge. It is not just cute anti-clock philosophy. It is the feeling that measured time and lived time are following different rules.

## Anecdote hooks
1. Lily's school had analog clocks at the front of the classroom, but the practical time source was a Chromebook or the Apple Watch kid.
2. The Apple Watch kid becomes the accidental authority: the truth strapped to someone's wrist.
3. The song turns "if nobody can read clocks, maybe time is just a myth" into the album's central joke and worry.
4. The violin makes the clock feel less like classroom furniture and more like something circling the room.
